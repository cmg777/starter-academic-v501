The Tension

Act I

Can a 64-number summary of a satellite photo tell you how a town is doing?

Bolivia has 339 municipalities. Many lack reliable survey data — yet every one of them is photographed from space.

A Google embedding model crushes each 2017 image into 64 numbers. Do those numbers know anything about human development?

This is the core promise of remote-sensing-for-development: imagery is cheap and global, surveys are expensive and sparse. The whole tutorial is a stress test of how much of that promise is real. Frame it as an honest empirical question, not a foregone conclusion.

Where we’re going

The Investigation

Act II

The target is tightly bunched: most towns score between 47 and 55

Distribution of IMDS across 339 municipalities; dashed = mean (51.1), dotted = median (50.5).

This bunching is the whole difficulty. With a standard deviation of only 6.77 on a 0–100 scale, there is little spread for a model to explain. The handful of towns above 70 are the big cities (La Paz, Santa Cruz, Cochabamba). A model that just guesses “about 51” is already hard to beat.

No single embedding is a smoking gun — the best correlations are only 0.25–0.40

Correlation matrix: the ten embedding dimensions most correlated with IMDS.

Moderate, not strong. That is typical for satellite-derived features against a complex socioeconomic outcome. Several embeddings also correlate with each other (overlapping spatial patterns) — multicollinearity that would trouble OLS but that a Random Forest shrugs off because it subsamples features at every split.

A Random Forest averages many decorrelated trees to cut variance

\[\hat{y} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} T_b(\mathbf{x})\]

Each tree \(T_b\) is grown on a bootstrap resample, and at every split only \(\sqrt{64}=8\) features are even considered.

Bootstrap rows + random feature subsets = trees that make different mistakes; averaging cancels the noise.

Walk the two sources of randomness aloud. Bagging (bootstrap aggregating) decorrelates by resampling rows; the random feature subset decorrelates further by hiding columns. Lower correlation between trees means a bigger variance reduction when you average — that is the entire trick. Here B is n_estimators (100 baseline, 500 tuned) and x is the 64-dimensional embedding vector.

Honesty first: split 339 into 271 train / 68 test before fitting anything

Train (271)

  • everything the model fits on
  • 5-fold CV runs inside here
  • tuning never sees the test set

Test (68)

  • touched once, at the very end
  • the honest generalization score
  • ~1.5% of data per prediction

Letting the model glimpse the test set during tuning is data leakage — it inflates the score and lies about generalization.

The discipline is the point of the lesson. With only 339 rows, a single 68-row test set could be unrepresentative by luck, which is exactly why 5-fold cross-validation inside the training set matters: it rotates the validation fold and averages, giving a steadier read before we ever spend the test set.

Five rotating exams give a steadier read than one lucky split

baseline_rf = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=100, random_state=RANDOM_SEED)
cv_scores = cross_val_score(baseline_rf, X_train, y_train, cv=5, scoring="r2")
# 5-fold CV R²: [0.152 0.187 0.270 0.308 0.345]
# Mean CV R² = 0.2526  (± 0.0728)

The fold-to-fold swing from 0.15 to 0.35 is the small-sample noise we are fighting.

The wide spread across folds (sd ≈ 0.07) is the small-n reality: 271 municipalities, sliced five ways, look quite different from each other. The CV mean of 0.2526 is our reliable baseline target — about a quarter of IMDS variance explained before any tuning.

Tuning buys 2 points of cross-validated R-squared: 0.2526 to 0.2721

param_distributions = {
    "n_estimators": [100, 200, 300, 500],
    "max_depth": [None, 10, 20, 30],
    "min_samples_leaf": randint(1, 5),
    "max_features": ["sqrt", "log2", None],
}
search = RandomizedSearchCV(
    RandomForestRegressor(random_state=RANDOM_SEED),
    param_distributions, n_iter=50, cv=5, scoring="r2",
)
search.fit(X_train, y_train)   # best CV R² = 0.2721 → 500 trees, max_depth=30

RandomizedSearchCV samples 50 random configurations rather than exhausting a grid — far cheaper and usually just as good. The winner deepens the trees (max_depth=30) but keeps max_features=“sqrt”, which is what preserves tree diversity. Hold the thought: this 2-point CV gain is about to evaporate on the test set.

A59, A42 and A26 carry the signal — but importance is spread thin

Top-20 embedding dimensions by permutation importance (drop in test R-squared when a feature is shuffled).

Permutation importance is the trustworthy method: shuffle one column on the test set, watch R² fall. A59 is the clear leader under both this and the impurity-based MDI ranking, with A42 and A26 close behind. But note how gently the bars decline — no single dimension dominates. The forest is reading many faint visual patterns at once, not one bright signal.

MDI and permutation disagree — trust the one measured on held-out data

MDI (impurity)

  • built into the fitted model, free
  • ranks A30 and A59 on top
  • biased toward high-cardinality features

Permutation

  • shuffles each feature on the test set
  • A59 on top; A30 drops sharply
  • unbiased by scale or cardinality

When the two rankings diverge, permutation wins — it measures predictive contribution, not split bookkeeping.

This is a teaching moment about feature-importance pitfalls. MDI counts how often a feature is used to split and how much impurity it removes, which rewards features with many distinct values. Permutation directly asks “does generalization suffer without you?” — a cleaner question for honest interpretation.

The relationships are non-linear thresholds — which is why a tree beats a line

Partial dependence for the top-6 embeddings: sharp rises then plateaus, not straight lines.

Partial dependence averages the prediction as one feature sweeps its range, holding the rest at their observed distribution. The step-like shapes — flat, then a sharp climb, then a plateau — are exactly what a linear regression would smear into a single slope. This justifies reaching for a Random Forest: it captures the rural-to-urban threshold without anyone hand-coding it.

The Resolution

Act III

On unseen towns the tuned forest explains about 23% of the variation

0.23

test R-squared (tuned: 0.2297, RMSE 6.52, MAE 4.72) — essentially equal to the untuned 0.2307

Here is the headline, and the twist: tuning moved CV R² up by 2 points but the held-out test R² actually nudged down (0.2307 → 0.2297). The 2-point CV gain did not transfer. That is the small-sample lesson in one line — chasing hyperparameters when the data has limited signal buys almost nothing.

The model is typically off by 4.7 IMDS points — and worst at the extremes

Residuals versus predicted IMDS: roughly centered on zero, but the spread widens at the edges.

MAE of 4.7 on a scale where most towns sit between 47 and 55 means the typical error is a meaningful slice of the whole spread. Residuals are centered (no gross bias) but fan out at the extremes — heteroscedasticity that matches the regression-to-the-mean we saw in the very first scatter. The model is confident about average towns and lost on unusual ones.

Tuning was a wash — proof the ceiling is the features, not the knobs

Metric Baseline Tuned
R-squared 0.2307 0.2297
RMSE 6.52 6.52
MAE 4.68 4.72

Default and tuned land on top of each other — when the signal is capped, configuration barely moves the needle.

This table is the punchline of the practical lesson. A common beginner instinct is to keep tuning; here it is empirically pointless. The honest read is that 64 embeddings carry roughly a quarter of IMDS variance, full stop. More trees and deeper splits cannot manufacture signal that is not in the features.

Did the forest overfit? No — and the gap to the data ceiling is the proof

Objection. A 500-tree, depth-30 forest on only 271 rows must be memorizing noise.

Response. If it were overfitting, CV and test R-squared would diverge wildly; instead both sit near 0.23–0.27, and bagging plus the \(\sqrt{64}\) feature subset cap variance by design. The model is under-powered by the features, not over-fit to the rows.

Steelman the worry — deep trees on small n is a real overfitting setup. The rebuttal is empirical: the small, stable gap between cross-validated and held-out performance is the signature of a model that generalizes about as well as it can. Random Forests are famously hard to overfit because averaging decorrelated trees shrinks variance. The constraint here is information, not capacity.

The 77% it misses lives off-camera: pair the pixels with survey data

  • Governance, migration, and informal economies are invisible from orbit
  • Satellite embeddings are a genuine but partial proxy — a starting layer
  • Next experiment: fuse embeddings with administrative or survey covariates

End on the constructive turn. The roughly 23% explained is not a failure — it is honest evidence that imagery captures real development structure. The roughly 77% unexplained tells you precisely where to look next: the human factors a camera cannot see. The natural follow-up is data fusion, not a fancier model.

Satellite pixels know the middle of the distribution — for the edges, you still need the survey.

The single takeaway. Imagery is a powerful, cheap first layer that explains about a quarter of municipal development and nails typical towns, but it regresses the extremes to the mean and tuning cannot rescue that. Treat embeddings as one input among several, not a replacement for ground truth.
