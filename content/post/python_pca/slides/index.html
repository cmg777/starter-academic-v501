The Tension

Act I

How do you rank 50 countries when health has many faces?

Life expectancy is in years. Infant mortality is a rate per 1,000. You cannot add years to rates.

And the two pull in opposite directions — more years is good, more deaths is bad. Which single number captures a country’s health?

The central tension of composite-index building in development economics: progress is rarely one metric, yet policymakers want one ranking. The indicators are in incompatible units and even point opposite ways. PCA is the disciplined way to compress them.

The two indicators tell one story — in opposite directions

Raw health indicators for 50 simulated countries: a very strong negative relationship, \(r = -0.96\).

Spoiler figure. Don’t unpack every dot — just plant that the cloud is a tight diagonal line. That tightness (r = -0.96) is exactly the redundancy PCA will exploit. Note the directional conflict we must fix first: higher life expectancy sits with lower infant mortality.

Where we’re going

  • Align indicator directions, then put them on a common scale
  • Measure how much the two indicators overlap
  • Let eigen-decomposition find the optimal blending weights
  • Project, rescale, and verify against scikit-learn

Teaching agenda built from the post’s learning objectives: polarity + standardization, covariance, eigen-decomposition, scoring + normalization, verification. Six steps, one composite index.

The Investigation

Act II

Six steps turn two raw indicators into one composite index

  • Step 1 — Polarity adjustment: flip “more is bad” indicators
  • Step 2 — Standardization: z-scores, mean 0, SD 1
  • Step 3 — Covariance matrix: measure the overlap
  • Step 4 — Eigen-decomposition: find the optimal direction
  • Step 5 — Scoring: project onto PC1
  • Step 6 — Normalization: rescale to \([0, 1]\)

No step can be skipped — each one feeds the next.

This is the spine of the whole talk. The mermaid pipeline in the post is this list. Standardization is the prerequisite that makes “years” and “rates” comparable; eigen-decomposition is the mathematical core.

Step 1 — flip infant mortality so “up” always means “better”

\[IM_i^{*} = -1 \times IM_i\]

Multiply the “more is bad” indicator by \(-1\) — now bigger always means healthier.

The raw correlation flips from \(-0.96\) to \(+0.96\); same relationship, properly aligned.

A large value of IM* now means low infant mortality — a good outcome. Analogy from the post: averaging exam scores where one professor grades 0–100 (higher better) and another grades demerits (lower better). You must flip the demerit scale first. Country_01’s infant mortality 18.6 becomes -18.6, now read as “18.6 units of infant survival.”

Step 2 — standardize so years and rates share one ruler

\[Z_{ij} = \frac{X_{ij} - \bar{X}_j}{\sigma_j}\]

Subtract the mean, divide by the SD — both indicators become mean 0, SD 1 and directly comparable.

We use the population SD (\(\sigma\), ddof=0): PCA treats the dataset as the full population.

The z-scores are now unitless, so years and rates share one ruler. Without this, infant mortality would dominate purely because its physical spread (3.5–58.7) is wider than life expectancy’s (54.9–84.7). After z-scoring, a country 2 SD above average in longevity is directly comparable to one 2 SD above in survival. Note describe() reports std 8.62 (ddof=1) vs 8.53 here (ddof=0) — same data, different denominator.

Step 3 — for standardized data, covariance is correlation

\[\Sigma = \frac{1}{n} Z^\top Z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & r \\ r & 1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Standardized data puts 1s on the diagonal and the correlation \(r\) off-diagonal.

Here the off-diagonal is \(r = 0.96\) — the two indicators move almost in lockstep.

With two variables, PCA only has to decompose this single 2×2 matrix. The covariance matrix quantifies the overlap and tells the algorithm: these two variables share a lot of redundant information, so you can compress them with little loss. 96% of the movement in one is mirrored by the other.

Step 4 — eigen-decomposition finds the direction of maximum spread

\[\Sigma \mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\]

Eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}\) = direction of greatest spread (the index weights). Eigenvalue \(\lambda\) = variance along it.

For a \(2\times2\) correlation matrix: \(\lambda_1 = 1 + r\), \(\lambda_2 = 1 - r\).

Imagine plotting all 50 countries with standardized life expectancy on x and infant survival on y — a narrow diagonal cloud. PC1 is a line through its long axis (maximum separation between countries); PC2 is perpendicular (countries bunch up, little information). Eigen-decomposition removes human bias: the math, not a guess, picks the optimal weights.

With \(r = 0.96\), PC1 absorbs almost all the variance: 97.97%

PC1 and PC2 eigenvector arrows over the standardized data — PC1 (orange) runs along the diagonal of maximum spread.

Closed-form: lambda_1 = 1 + 0.9595 = 1.9595, lambda_2 = 1 - 0.9595 = 0.0405. Variance explained by PC1 = 1.9595 / 2 = 97.97%. The orange PC1 arrow runs diagonally; the teal PC2 arrow is perpendicular and short, capturing only the 2% residual. Higher correlation means more variance on PC1.

A single number keeps 98% of the information

PC1 captures 98.0% of total variance; PC2 captures just 2.0%.

The eigenvalues sum to 2 (the number of variables). PC1’s near-total dominance confirms heavy redundancy — the ideal scenario for PCA compression. The remaining 2% on PC2 is mostly simulation noise. At zero correlation both eigenvalues would equal 1 and PCA would buy you nothing.

Two standardized variables always get equal weights — 0.7071 each

Component \(w_1\) (LE) \(w_2\) (IM*) Variance
PC1 0.7071 0.7071 97.97%
PC2 0.7071 −0.7071 2.03%

\(0.7071 \approx 1/\sqrt{2}\) — a mathematical certainty for any two standardized variables, regardless of \(r\).

This is the key teaching point. A 2x2 correlation matrix always has eigenvectors [1/sqrt2, 1/sqrt2] and [1/sqrt2, -1/sqrt2] — the correlation only changes the eigenvalues (how much variance), never the weights. So PC1 here is just a scaled average of the two z-scores. With 3+ variables the weights diverge, and that is where PCA earns its keep.

Step 5 — project each country onto PC1 to get its score

\[PC1_i = w_1 Z_{i,LE} + w_2 Z_{i,IM}\]

The eigenvector is the recipe — multiply each z-score by its weight and sum.

w1, w2 = eigenvectors[0, 0], eigenvectors[1, 0]   # 0.7071, 0.7071
df["pc1"] = w1 * df["z_le"] + w2 * df["z_im"]      # project onto PC1

Two variables officially become one composite number. Geometrically: drop each country’s 2D dot squarely onto the diagonal PC1 line; its position along the line is its health score. Country_01: 0.70711.04 + 0.70710.76 = 1.27 — well above the mean of 0, healthier half of the sample. Scores range -2.39 to +2.37.

PC1 scores rank every country on one health axis

50 countries ranked by PC1 score — teal above average, orange below, roughly symmetric around zero.

The top scorers pair high life expectancy (81–85 years) with very low infant mortality (3.5–8.6 per 1,000); the bottom scorers show the opposite. Mean is exactly 0 (standardized inputs). But negative scores are hard to communicate in a policy report — which motivates Step 6.

Step 6 — rescale to [0, 1] so a policymaker can read it

\[HI_i = \frac{PC1_i - PC1_{\min}}{PC1_{\max} - PC1_{\min}}\]

Min-max scaling: worst → 0, best → 1, everyone proportional between.

Country_01’s PC1 of 1.27 becomes a Health Index of 0.77 — “better than 77% of the scale.”

You cannot publish “this country scores -2.39.” Normalization forces the worst to 0 and the best to 1, producing a purely data-driven index that is instantly understandable. The mean Health Index is 0.50 — close to 0.5 by symmetry.

The Health Index reveals a stark three-tier health divide

Health Index for 50 countries, orange (worst) to teal (best). Country_05 sits at exactly 0.00.

Countries cluster into three groups: high above 0.75, a middle band, and a struggling cluster below 0.25. The bottom 10 all fall below 0.16 — systemic weakness across both longevity and infant survival, candidates for comprehensive health packages. Country_05 has no visible bar: it’s the minimum, mapped to exactly 0.00 by definition.

The Resolution

Act III

PC1 captures 97.97% of the variance — almost lossless compression

97.97%

variance retained by a single composite index built from two correlated indicators

The answer to the opening question: yes, PCA collapses two correlated health indicators into one Health Index that keeps virtually all the information. The compression is near-lossless precisely because r = 0.96.

The manual pipeline matches scikit-learn to machine precision

Manual vs scikit-learn PC1 scores: all 50 points fall on the 45-degree line of perfect agreement.

Max absolute difference 1.33e-15 — machine-precision zero. Correlation 1.000000. One subtlety: eigenvectors are defined up to a sign flip, so sklearn may return opposite-sign scores; we check the correlation and flip if needed. The six-step manual pipeline collapses to ~15 lines of sklearn, reusable by changing only the CSV path and the positive/negative column lists.

Does an equal-weight average make PCA pointless here? No

Objection. With two standardized variables the weights are forced to 0.7071 each, so PC1 is just a scaled average — why bother with eigen-decomposition?

Response. True in the two-variable case. But the machinery is what scales: with 15 or 20 indicators PCA discovers unequal weights that reflect each variable’s unique contribution — something no manual averaging scheme can find. The two-variable case is a transparent warm-up, not the destination.

Steelman the objection honestly. The post is explicit: equal weights are a 2-variable certainty; the real power emerges in higher dimensions. Reference Mendez & Gonzales (2021): PCA over malnutrition, language barriers, dropout, and education inequality across 339 Bolivian municipalities — there the weights genuinely differ.

The index measures relative performance — read it with care

  • A score of 1.0 means best in this sample, not perfect health
  • Adding or removing countries shifts every score (z-scores and min-max bounds both move)
  • A health index of 0.77 is not comparable to an education index of 0.77 — different eigenvectors, different scales
  • Year-by-year comparison needs a pooled PCA to hold the weights fixed

The post’s discussion caveats. Relativity is the big one: every score depends on which countries are in the sample. Cross-domain and over-time comparisons require extra methodological choices — pooled PCA, or the kind of normalization the UNDP HDI uses.

Highly correlated indicators compress almost losslessly — but PCA earns its keep in high dimensions.

The single takeaway. Two correlated indicators collapse to one number with 98% of the information retained and equal weights — a clean teaching case. The method’s real value is the same machinery applied to 15 or 20 indicators, where it discovers the weights you could never guess.
