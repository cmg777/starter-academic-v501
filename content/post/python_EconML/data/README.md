# Data dictionary - Causal Machine Learning and the Resource Curse

Companion data for a hands-on Python tutorial that estimates <strong>heterogeneous causal effects</strong> of mining and mineral prices on economic development using <strong>EconML's <code>CausalForestDML</code></strong> &mdash; a Double Machine Learning causal forest. The dataset is <strong>fully synthetic</strong> with a known data-generating process, so estimates can be checked against ground truth. Its structure mirrors Hodler, Lechner &amp; Raschky (2023): 300 districts across 8 countries observed annually over 2003&ndash;2012 (3,000 district-year rows). Treatment has four levels &mdash; no mining (0) and mining at low (1), medium (2), and high (3) mineral prices &mdash; and is heavily imbalanced at 85%/5%/5%/5%. The outcome is log nighttime lights. The forest recovers an ATE of 0.240 for the basic mining effect (true 0.250), a non-linear price gradient, and the finding that institutions moderate the mining margin but not the price margin. The entire DGP is open and reproducible.


Companion data for the post <https://carlos-mendez.org/post/python_EconML/>. Each dataset ships as a labeled Stata `.dta` (v118) plus its source file. Generated by `build_data_dictionary.py`.

## Known limitations & caveats

- <strong>Synthetic data.</strong> There is no real data behind this tutorial; results are internally consistent with the calibration but are not empirical evidence about real-world mining, prices, or development.
- <strong>Heavily imbalanced treatment.</strong> 85% of rows are untreated (no mining) and only 5% fall in each mining level (150 rows each); within-mining price contrasts (e.g. 3-1) draw on just 300 observations, so price-effect standard errors are large.
- <strong>No clustered standard errors.</strong> EconML's <code>inference=True</code> reports forest-level Bootstrap-of-Little-Bags SEs that treat observations as independent; with this panel (same district across years) true clustered SEs would typically be larger. <code>GroupKFold</code> by district prevents first-stage leakage but does not cluster second-stage variance.
- <strong>Contemporaneous outcomes.</strong> Treatment and outcome are measured in the same year; the structural template (Hodler, Lechner &amp; Raschky 2023) lags the outcome to <code>t+1</code> to rule out reverse causality.
- <strong>Identification rests on the CIA.</strong> The Conditional Independence Assumption holds here by construction; in real data it is untestable and a causal forest does not relax it.
