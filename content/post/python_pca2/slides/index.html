The Tension

Act I

If the ruler changes between measurements, you cannot tell whether the object grew or the ruler shrank

We want one composite Human Development Index for 153 South American regions, comparable across 2013 and 2019.

But running PCA separately each period lets the weights and the standardization drift. Did development change, or did the yardstick?

This is the central problem of building composite indicators over time. The measuring instrument itself must stay fixed. If you re-fit PCA each period, both the eigenvector weights and the z-score parameters (means, SDs) move — a region’s index can rise simply because the sample’s average fell.

Education and health rose, but income fell — a mixed signal a naive method erases

2013 (steel) vs 2019 (orange). The teal arrow connects the period centroids: the cloud shifts right (education up) but down (income down).

Spoiler figure. Across South America, education rose +0.0225 and health +0.0134, but income declined −0.0202 — driven by collapses such as Venezuela’s income falling from 0.782 to 0.630. This mixed signal is exactly what makes the pooled-vs-per-period choice consequential. We earn the rest in Act II.

Where we’re going

  • The data: a 153-region × 2-period panel of three HDI sub-indices
  • The problem: per-period PCA produces non-comparable scores
  • The fix: pooled standardization, weights, and bounds from stacked data
  • The payoff: a fixed yardstick that validates against the official SHDI

Act II

Per-period PCA re-fits the weights every period — the index formula itself drifts

PC1 eigenvector weights shift between 2013 and 2019: education’s weight drops, health’s jumps.

The 2013 weights are [0.583, 0.510, 0.632]; the 2019 weights are [0.541, 0.566, 0.623]. Education’s weight drops −0.043 while health’s jumps +0.056. So a region’s 2013 HDI and 2019 HDI are computed with different recipes — temporal comparison is unreliable before we even reach the data.

Re-centring each period to zero erases the net signal — 43 regions appear to decline

Per-period PCA: the 10 largest rank shifts. Teal = improved, orange = declined.

Per-period standardization forces each period’s mean to zero, so the net positive shift in education and health is invisible by construction. 43 of 153 regions appear to decline despite the overall improvement. Running example: City of Buenos Aires shows a per-period HDI fall of −0.04 (ranked #1 in 2013) — yet its education and health both rose. Is it really declining, or is the shifting ruler hiding the story?

Pooled standardization measures every region against one fixed ruler

\[Z_{ij,t}^{\text{pool}}=\frac{X_{ij,t}-\bar{X}_j^{\text{pool}}}{\sigma_j^{\text{pool}}}\]

The pooled mean \(\bar{X}_j^{\text{pool}}\) and SD \(\sigma_j^{\text{pool}}\) are computed from all 306 rows at once, then applied to every observation in every period.

Per-period shifts the baseline each year; pooled fixes it — so 0.926 → 0.946 is a genuine z-score rise.

Analogy: measuring children at age 5 and age 10. Per-period compares each child only to same-age peers — you can’t see growth because the reference group changes. Pooled measures everyone against the combined distribution, so the z-score increase reflects actual growth. Without it, the income decline (0.736 → 0.715) would be re-centred away.

One eigen-decomposition on the stacked data yields a single, fixed set of weights

\[\Sigma^{\text{pool}}\,\mathbf{v}_k=\lambda_k\,\mathbf{v}_k,\qquad PC1_{i,t}=\sum_{j} w_j\,Z_{ij,t}^{\text{pool}}\]

Pooled eigenvalues \([2.173,\,0.563,\,0.264]\); PC1 weights \([0.564,\,0.545,\,0.620]\) — the same recipe for 2013 and 2019.

Because the weights come from the pooled data, the index formula no longer drifts between periods.

Income carries the heaviest weight (0.620), then education (0.564), then health (0.545). With three variables, PCA discovers data-driven weights — unlike the two-variable case where equal weights were a mathematical certainty. Crucially, these weights are fixed across time.

scikit-learn confirms it: fit_transform on stacked data is pooled PCA

from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
scaler = StandardScaler()              # POOLED: fit on ALL 306 rows
Z = scaler.fit_transform(df[cols])
pca = PCA(n_components=1)              # one set of weights for both periods
df["pc1"] = pca.fit_transform(Z)[:, 0]

No special panel-data library is needed — pooling is simply fitting the scaler and PCA on the stacked frame. The sklearn weights [0.5642, 0.5448, 0.6204] and variance ratio 0.7242 match the from-scratch NumPy implementation to 2e-15. Validation against the manual code is the reproducibility check.

PC1 captures 72.4% of variance — strong, but real data is not one-dimensional

Variance explained: PC1 72.4%, PC2 18.8%, PC3 8.8%.

72.4% is a strong majority but well below the 96% in the simulated tutorial. We focus on PC1 as the composite HDI, but PC2 may carry policy-relevant structure.

The Resolution

Act III

The two methods disagree on the direction of change for 16 of 153 regions

Pooled vs per-period HDI change. The dashed 45° line is perfect agreement; most points sit below it.

The cloud sits systematically below the line: per-period PCA understates improvement (or overstates decline) because it erases the net positive shift in education and health. In all 16 disagreement cases, per-period shows a decline while pooled shows an improvement — a policymaker reading per-period PCA could wrongly conclude these regions are “falling behind.”

High rank agreement, but consequential re-orderings: Spearman ρ = 0.982

Improvement-rank comparison. Crossing lines mark where the two methods re-order who improved most.

Spearman ρ = 0.9818 on improvement rankings — the methods largely agree on who improved most. But the crossing lines show specific re-orderings: regions whose gains were partly masked by the shifting per-period baseline get ranked lower under per-period PCA. High aggregate agreement does not rule out consequential individual differences.

Validated against the official SHDI, pooled PCA wins on both levels and dynamics

Comparison Pooled \(R^2\) Per-period \(R^2\)
Levels (vs SHDI) 0.9823 0.9750
Changes (vs SHDI) 0.9964 0.9913

The official SHDI uses a fixed geometric-mean formula across years — so it tracks the fixed-yardstick pooled index more tightly.

Pooled PCA correlates r = 0.991 with the official SHDI (R² = 0.9823) vs 0.9750 for per-period. For changes over time the gap holds: 0.9964 vs 0.9913. Both differences are small but consistent and in the same direction — when the goal is temporal comparability, fitting on stacked data is the right choice.

Left panel: pooled PCA points from both periods intermix seamlessly along the fit line — exactly what a temporally comparable index should do. Right panel: per-period PCA scatters wider, reflecting the distortion from re-centring each period to its own baseline.

Tracking dynamics is the real test — pooled change fits the SHDI change almost exactly

SHDI change validation: pooled (left, R² = 0.996) falls almost on the line; per-period (right, R² = 0.991) disperses more.

The change validation is more compelling than the level validation. Both methods track SHDI dynamics well (r > 0.99), but pooled is the tighter fit (0.9964 vs 0.9913). Since the whole promise of pooled PCA is capturing dynamics, this is the test that matters most.

A fixed classification makes the map honest: 40 regions climbed, 25 fell

Pooled-PCA HDI choropleths, 2013 vs 2019, with Fisher-Jenks breaks fixed from 2013. Any colour change is a real threshold crossing.

Holding the 2013 Fisher-Jenks breaks constant in the 2019 map is essential — a region changes colour only if it genuinely crossed a development threshold. 40 regions moved up a class, 88 stayed, 25 declined. Upward mobility concentrates in the Andes (Peru, Bolivia, Colombia); the declines are predominantly Venezuelan states — a cartographic echo of the economic crisis.

Education converged, but income and health diverged — overall inequality rose

Gini index by indicator, 2013 vs 2019. Education’s Gini falls; health and income rise.

Education is the only dimension that converged (Gini 0.0655 → 0.0639). Health (+0.0023) and income (+0.0036) diverged; the composite HDI Gini rose 0.1712 → 0.1795. So development progress is not uniform across dimensions — South America narrowed educational gaps while income setbacks (especially Venezuela) widened. A composite index captures these cross-cutting dynamics in one number.

Does PCA make the index “true”? No — it makes it comparable

Objection. PC1 discards 28% of the variance and the weights are sample-specific — adding or removing regions changes every score.

Response. Correct, and worth stating plainly. Pooled PCA does not claim a true index — it guarantees a fixed yardstick across time. PC2 (19%) may hold real structure; the index is relative to this sample. What pooling buys is comparability, validated at \(R^2 = 0.98\) against the official SHDI.

Steelman, don’t strawman. PC1 captures 72% of variance, so 28% is lost in compression. The pooled approach also assumes a stable correlation structure between 2013 and 2019 — a strong assumption over a volatile 6-year window. The contribution is methodological comparability, not a claim that PCA weights are the “right” weights.

Fit the standardization and the weights on the stacked data, and the yardstick stops moving.

The single takeaway. Whenever a composite indicator must be compared across time, pool: one standardization, one set of weights, one normalization — computed once from all periods together. That is the difference between seeing +0.144 of real progress and zeroing it out by construction.
