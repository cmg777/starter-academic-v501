# Data dictionary - Do Industrial Parks Work? Evaluating Place-Based Policy in Ethiopia with Difference-in-Differences

Companion data for a beginner-friendly, end-to-end staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) tutorial that asks whether Ethiopia's industrial parks raised local economic activity, household living standards, and women's economic agency &mdash; and for whom. The tutorial replicates <strong>Huang, Wang &amp; Xu (2026)</strong> on <strong>fully synthetic</strong> data spanning three grains: a satellite district-year panel of 139 woredas (2005&ndash;2020), and two Ethiopia DHS repeated cross-sections of households and individuals across five survey rounds. The estimand throughout is the <strong>average treatment effect on the treated (ATT)</strong> &mdash; the effect on the 17 park-hosting woredas relative to 122 propensity-score-matched never-treated controls &mdash; identified under parallel trends in an explicitly observational setting. The analysis runs a static two-way fixed-effects DiD and an event study with <code>pyfixest</code>, cross-checks them against the Sun-Abraham, Borusyak/Gardner and Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators plus a Goodman-Bacon decomposition, and runs survey-weighted repeated-cross-section DiD with Conley spatial standard errors.


Companion data for the post <https://carlos-mendez.org/post/python_did_industrial_park/>. Each dataset ships as a labeled Stata `.dta` (v118) plus its source file. Generated by `build_data_dictionary.py`.

## Known limitations & caveats

- <strong>Synthetic data.</strong> The three CSVs are 100% synthetic, built for teaching. They are calibrated to reproduce the paper's findings, not the paper's real (confidential) inputs — harmonized nighttime lights, the GISD30 impervious-surface product, Ethiopia DHS micro-data, and the official park list. Use them to learn the methods, not to draw conclusions about Ethiopia.
- <strong>Tiny treated group.</strong> Only 17 treated woredas against 122 matched controls, so several effects are borderline and the small treated sample cannot make all moderators precise at once (e.g. the primary-road interaction is correctly signed but insignificant).
- <strong>Observational, not randomized.</strong> Parks were not randomly placed (they went to denser, more urban, better-connected woredas), so identification rests on parallel trends, not randomization; the fixed effects and trend terms are confounding controls, not precision-only adjustments.
- <strong>Documented synthetic gaps.</strong> The raw-light coefficient runs high (~1.6 vs the paper's 1.276, a bright-base artifact); light levels are intentionally not PSM-matched (the EDA figure is baseline-normalized); the decision-power mean sits a touch below the paper's because of a linear-probability clipping ceiling. See the post's Section 13 reproduction audit.
- <strong>Repeated cross-sections.</strong> The household and individual files have no within-respondent panel key, so they admit only coarse event phases and survey-weighted regressions, never unit fixed effects.
