# Data dictionary - Carbon Taxes and CO2 Emissions: Sweden as a Case Study

Companion data for a Python tutorial that replicates <strong>Andersson (2019, <em>AEJ: Economic Policy</em>)</strong> on whether Sweden's 1991 carbon tax cut transport CO<sub>2</sub> emissions &mdash; and at what economic cost. The headline dataset is an OECD panel of 15 advanced economies observed over 1960&ndash;2005 (46 years; 30 pre-treatment, 16 post-treatment), measuring per-capita CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from transport in metric tons alongside the predictors used to build the counterfactual (GDP per capita, vehicles, gasoline consumption, urbanisation, population density). The post layers a naive before/after comparison, difference-in-differences, and the synthetic-control method (<code>pysyncon</code>) &mdash; validated by in-time, in-space, and leave-one-out placebo tests &mdash; then OLS/IV demand regressions (<code>pyfixest</code>). Synthetic Sweden, built from six donors (Denmark, Belgium, New Zealand, Greece, United States, Switzerland), implies an average 11.3% annual reduction over 1990&ndash;2005 with no measurable growth penalty. A second file carries the three counterfactual emission paths used to disentangle the carbon tax from the bundled VAT.


Companion data for the post <https://carlos-mendez.org/post/python_sc_co2tax/>. Each dataset ships as a labeled Stata `.dta` (v118) plus its source file. Generated by `build_data_dictionary.py`.

## Known limitations & caveats

- <strong>Single-country case.</strong> Sweden is one treated unit; external validity to larger emitters or developing economies is not guaranteed.
- <strong>Donor-pool size caps the p-value.</strong> With 15 countries the smallest possible permutation p-value is 1/15 &asymp; 0.067 — exactly the value the post hits.
- <strong>Coverage gaps.</strong> <code>GDP_per_capita</code> is missing for 10 of 690 country-year rows (688 present); the synthetic-control predictors are averaged over windows where data exist.
- <strong>Simulated counterfactual paths.</strong> The three <code>*CarbonTax*</code>/<code>*VAT*</code> columns in <code>disentangling_data</code> are model-simulated emission scenarios (defined only from 1970 onward), not raw observations; they normalise differently from the synthetic-control baseline, so the carbon-tax-only share (≈9.5% here) and Andersson's headline (6.3%) describe the same physical wedge under different denominators.
- <strong>Window ends in 2005.</strong> The panel predates the electric-vehicle surge and later EU climate policy; re-running with newer data would test persistence.
