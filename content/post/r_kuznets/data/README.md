# Data dictionary - Spatial Inequality and the Kuznets Curve

Companion data for a hands-on R tutorial that replicates Lessmann (2014) on a <strong>fully synthetic</strong> dataset of regional GDP per capita for 56 countries over 1980&ndash;2009. The post measures spatial inequality with the population-weighted coefficient of variation (WCV) of regional income and estimates the spatial Kuznets curve &mdash; the hypothesis that regional inequality first rises then falls as development increases &mdash; using cross-section OLS, two-way fixed effects (fixest), and semiparametric methods (Robinson 1988, Baltagi&ndash;Li 2002). The inverted-U is robust across estimators; the high-income upturn (N-shape) appears only in cross-country comparisons, not within countries over time. The entire data-generating process is open and reproducible.


Companion data for the post <https://carlos-mendez.org/post/r_kuznets/>. Each dataset ships as a labeled Stata `.dta` (v118) plus its source file. Generated by `build_data_dictionary.py`.

## Known limitations & caveats

- <strong>Synthetic data.</strong> There is no real data behind this tutorial; results are internally consistent with the calibration but are not empirical evidence about real-world spatial inequality.
- <strong>Fragile N-shape.</strong> The high-income upturn (cubic term) appears with income in logs and weakens in levels; the robust finding is the inverted-U, not the N-shape.
- <strong>Within vs. between.</strong> The genuine N-shape is cross-sectional (between countries); the within-country cubic term is insignificant.
- <strong>Significance ≠ shape.</strong> All income terms can be significant yet the discriminant D = β₂² − 3·β₁·β₃ be negative or the turning points fall outside the observed range — always check D > 0 and that turning points lie in range.
