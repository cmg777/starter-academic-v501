# Data dictionary - What Does TWFE Actually Do? Manual Demeaning and the FWL Theorem

Companion data for a hands-on R tutorial that takes the <strong>two-way fixed effects</strong> (TWFE) estimator apart to show it is nothing more than ordinary least squares applied to <strong>two-way demeaned</strong> data &mdash; the equivalence guaranteed by the <strong>Frisch&ndash;Waugh&ndash;Lovell (FWL) theorem</strong>. The tutorial uses a balanced, <strong>synthetic</strong> Barro convergence panel of 150 countries observed over 8 time periods (1,200 observations), regressing GDP-per-capita growth on log initial income, investment share, population growth, human capital, and government consumption. It estimates the model with country and time fixed effects via <code>fixest::feols()</code>, then replicates the coefficients by hand &mdash; subtracting country means, subtracting time means, and adding back the grand mean before running base R&apos;s <code>lm()</code>. The two routes match to at least 12 significant digits (the convergence coefficient is &minus;0.055286 either way; the largest coefficient difference is 3.05&times;10<sup>&minus;16</sup>, on the order of machine epsilon), while naive <code>lm()</code> standard errors understate uncertainty by 7&ndash;22% because they ignore the degrees of freedom consumed by the absorbed fixed effects.


Companion data for the post <https://carlos-mendez.org/post/r_demeaning_twfe/>. Each dataset ships as a labeled Stata `.dta` (v118) plus its source file. Generated by `build_data_dictionary.py`.

## Known limitations & caveats

- <strong>Synthetic data.</strong> The panel is simulated; coefficient values reflect the data-generating process, not real-world economic dynamics — they are not empirical evidence about growth convergence.
- <strong>Coefficients match, standard errors do not.</strong> OLS on the demeaned data reproduces the TWFE point estimates exactly, but naive <code>lm()</code> standard errors are too small (by 7–22% here) because they do not subtract the degrees of freedom consumed by the 150 country plus 8 time effects. Always use a dedicated panel estimator (e.g. <code>feols()</code>) for inference.
- <strong>Balanced panel only.</strong> The closed-form two-way demeaning used here is exact because the panel is balanced; unbalanced panels require the iterative projection that <code>fixest</code> performs internally.
- <strong>Demeaning kills time-invariant regressors.</strong> Any variable constant within a country over time equals its own country mean, so its demeaned column is zero everywhere and it drops out — this is why fixed-effects models cannot identify time-invariant characteristics.
