# Pedagogy Conventions

The apps generated by this skill must help advanced undergraduates and
early graduate students build intuition. These conventions are the
non-negotiable design principles, applied in Phase 3 when generating
markup and copy.

---

## 1. Every widget has three copy elements

- **Lede** (above the widget): 1–3 sentences naming the trade-off the
  widget illustrates. End with a verb the student will do: "drag the
  slider", "toggle outcomes", "run 100 simulations".
- **The widget itself**: chart + controls.
- **"What to look for"** panel (below or beside): 2–4 bullets, each
  pointing at a specific observation the student should make. The
  bullets use the second-person imperative ("Slide right and see…",
  "Compare the two cards…"). At least one bullet should reference the
  post's central takeaway.

No widget should ship without all three.

---

## 2. Lede style

- Begin with the *empirical question*, not the method's name.
- Avoid jargon in the first sentence. Jargon allowed once defined.
- Concrete before abstract: "Suppose you have 284 controls" beats
  "in high-dimensional settings".
- End with the student's verb. The widget should feel like a tool
  the student is invited to use, not a chart they are shown.

**Example (LASSO Lab):**

> The simulated data has one treatment variable and many candidate
> controls. The true treatment coefficient is α = 0.5 (orange curve
> below). The LASSO chooses how many controls to keep based on a
> single penalty parameter λ. **Drag the λ slider and watch the
> coefficients shrink to exactly zero, one at a time.**

---

## 3. "What to look for" style

Three bullets is the sweet spot. Each bullet is **one specific
observation**, not a general statement.

**Bad:** "Notice how the coefficients change with λ."
**Good:** "Slide λ right past the dashed line — about 35 coefficients
disappear at once. Slide left to bring them back."

Use `<strong>` for the noun the student should fixate on
("Sparsity", "Post-OLS", "Treatment column"). Match the dark theme's
emphasis color via `--teal` in CSS.

---

## 4. Glossary cards (collapsible details/summary)

Every app's Intro tab includes a 6–10 entry glossary at the bottom.
Each entry uses `<details class="gloss">`:

```html
<details class="gloss"><summary>Term</summary>
<div class="gloss-body">Definition. <em>One-sentence example or
analogy in italics.</em></div>
</details>
```

Source the glossary in this order:

1. The post's own "Key concepts at a glance" section, if present.
2. If absent, generate 6 entries from the topic family's standard
   vocabulary (e.g. for `causal-inference`: ATE, ATT, confounder,
   identification, post-OLS, LASSO).

---

## 5. Performance caps and snappy interactions

A slider tick that takes more than ~300 ms feels broken. Caps are
chosen to keep every interaction under that budget on a 5-year-old
laptop:

- `n ≤ 500` (sample size)
- `p ≤ 100` (number of controls)
- `nLam ≤ 80` (path resolution)
- `nfolds = 3` for CV (matches the post's R code)
- Monte Carlo runs ≤ 100, batched 2 per tick with `setTimeout(0)`
  yields, with a visible progress bar.

If a widget's natural use cap is higher (e.g. spatial map with 500
regions), implement progressive rendering — show a coarse version
immediately, refine on idle. Never make the user wait.

---

## 6. Color encoding

Be consistent across apps. The site palette assigns colors *to
concepts*, not arbitrarily:

| Concept                                              | Color    |
|------------------------------------------------------|----------|
| Primary data / steel reference / "First diff" method | `--steel` |
| Treatment / orange highlight / "CV penalty" method   | `--orange`|
| Selected / "Rigorous penalty" / kept-coefficient     | `--teal`  |
| Muted / inactive / zeroed-coefficient                | `--muted` |
| Page background                                      | `--bg`    |
| Card background                                      | `--panel` |

The forest-plot color map for the 5 estimators is fixed
(`templates/widgets/forest-plot.js`); do not reassign.

---

## 7. Accessibility

- Every slider has an `aria-label`.
- Every button has visible text (no icon-only buttons).
- Touch targets are ≥ 44×44 px on mobile.
- Color contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 for body text (`--text` on `--bg` is
  ~14:1 ✓).
- Charts include text labels for non-visual readers (D3 `<text>`
  elements with axis titles).
- No autoplaying audio or video.

---

## 8. Mobile responsiveness

- The 4-tab nav bar must wrap or scroll horizontally on narrow
  viewports.
- 2-column grids collapse to 1 column under 900 px (already in
  `styles.css`).
- Charts use `viewBox` + `preserveAspectRatio` so they scale.

---

## 9. Tone and voice

- Second person ("you"), not third person.
- Active voice. "LASSO selects" beats "Controls are selected by LASSO".
- Avoid academic hedging ("it could be argued", "one might note").
  Apps are for action, not commentary.
- Reference the post once per tab; never assume the student read it.

---

## 10. The footer

Every app's footer says, in order:

1. One line citing the post (italic title).
2. The tech stack (D3.js v7).
3. A reassuring line: "No backend, no tracking."
4. A "Back to the post" link.

Keep it short. The footer is not a place to advertise.
